## What Is a Sewer Camera Inspection?

A sewer camera inspection uses a specialized waterproof camera attached to a flexible cable to visually examine the inside of your drain and sewer pipes. The camera transmits real-time video to a monitor, allowing technicians to see exactly what's happening inside your plumbing system.

### How the Technology Works

Modern sewer cameras feature:

- **HD video quality**: Clear images that reveal even small problems
- **LED lighting**: Illuminates the dark interior of pipes
- **Flexible cables**: Navigate bends and turns in your plumbing
- **Location transmitters**: Pinpoint exact positions of problems
- **Recording capability**: Document findings for your records and insurance

### Types of Sewer Cameras

- **Push cameras**: Manually guided through pipes, ideal for most residential inspections
- **Crawler cameras**: Self-propelled units for larger commercial lines
- **Lateral launch cameras**: Can inspect branch lines from the main sewer

## When You Need a Sewer Camera Inspection

### Warning Signs That Indicate Problems

**Call for an inspection if you experience:**

- **Frequent drain clogs** that keep returning after clearing
- **Multiple slow drains** throughout the house
- **Gurgling sounds** from toilets or drains
- **Sewage odors** inside or outside your home
- **Wet spots in your yard** especially along the sewer line path
- **Toilet bubbling** when you run water elsewhere
- **Sewage backups** in lower-level drains

## What Problems Sewer Cameras Find

### Root Intrusion

Tree roots seek water sources and can infiltrate sewer lines through tiny cracks or joint gaps. Once inside, roots grow and expand, eventually blocking or breaking pipes.

**Signs of root problems:**
- Recurring clogs in the same location
- Slow drains that worsen seasonally
- Gurgling sounds as water fights past obstructions

### Pipe Damage and Deterioration

**Cracks and fractures**: Can result from ground movement, age, or impact damage

**Corrosion**: Cast iron pipes eventually rust and deteriorate from the inside out

**Bellied pipes**: Sections that sag due to soil conditions, creating low spots where waste collects

**Collapsed sections**: Complete pipe failure requiring replacement

### Blockages and Buildup

**Grease accumulation**: Cooking grease solidifies in pipes over time

**Scale buildup**: Mineral deposits narrow pipe diameter

**Foreign objects**: Items that shouldn't be flushed causing obstructions

**Sediment accumulation**: Dirt and debris collecting in bellied sections

### Joint and Connection Issues

**Offset joints**: Pipes that have shifted out of alignment

**Separated joints**: Gaps where pipe sections have pulled apart

**Failed seals**: Deteriorated gaskets allowing infiltration

### Pipe Material Assessment

Cameras reveal what your pipes are made of:

- **Cast iron**: Common in homes before 1970s, prone to corrosion
- **Clay/terracotta**: Very old systems, brittle and root-prone
- **Orangeburg**: Compressed tar paper pipes from 1940s-1970s, often failing
- **PVC**: Modern plastic, durable but can crack
- **ABS**: Another modern plastic option

## The Inspection Process

### What to Expect

**1. Access Point Identification**
The technician locates a suitable access pointâ€”typically a cleanout, roof vent, or toilet removal point.

**2. Camera Insertion**
The camera is carefully fed into the pipe system, navigating through the lines.

**3. Real-Time Viewing**
You can watch the monitor as the camera travels through your pipes, seeing exactly what the technician sees.

**4. Problem Documentation**
Any issues are recorded with video and photos, noting the type of problem and its location.

**5. Location Marking**
For problems requiring repair, a transmitter pinpoints the exact location above ground.

**6. Report Generation**
You receive a detailed report including video footage, findings, and recommendations.

### Duration

Most residential inspections take 30-60 minutes, depending on:
- Length of sewer line
- Number of access points
- Complexity of the plumbing system
- Problems encountered

### Preparation Required

- Ensure access to cleanouts
- Know the approximate location of your sewer line
- Have previous inspection reports if available
- Clear access to relevant areas

## Sewer Inspections for Home Buyers

### Why Buyers Need Sewer Inspections

Standard home inspections don't include sewer line evaluation. Yet sewer problems can cost tens of thousands to repair. A pre-purchase sewer inspection protects your investment.

### What Buyers Learn

- **Current condition**: Is the system healthy or failing?
- **Remaining lifespan**: How long before major repairs are needed?
- **Pipe materials**: Are there problematic materials like Orangeburg?
- **Root threats**: Are trees threatening the line?
- **Previous repairs**: Evidence of patches or replacements

### Negotiating Power

Inspection findings can help you:

- **Negotiate price reductions** for needed repairs
- **Request seller repairs** before closing
- **Budget accurately** for future maintenance
- **Walk away** from properties with serious issues

### When to Inspect

- **Before waiving contingencies**: Make it part of your due diligence
- **For homes over 20 years old**: Higher likelihood of issues
- **Properties with large trees**: Root intrusion risk
- **Homes with history of backups**: Previous owners may have ignored problems

## Maintaining Your Sewer Line

Proper maintenance extends the life of your sewer system and prevents emergencies.

### Preventive Practices

**Watch what goes down:**
- Never pour grease or oil down drains
- Avoid flushing anything except toilet paper
- Use drain screens to catch debris
- Don't use harsh chemical drain cleaners

**Tree management:**
- Know where your sewer line runs
- Consider root barriers near vulnerable pipes
- Remove trees that repeatedly cause problems
- Choose sewer-safe landscaping

**Regular maintenance:**
- Professional cleaning every 1-2 years for older systems
- Camera inspections every 3-5 years
- Address slow drains promptly
- Know your cleanout locations

**Learn more:** [Essential Tips for Maintaining Your Sewer Line: What Homeowners Must Know](/essential-tips-for-maintaining-your-sewer-line-what-homeowners-must-know/)

### Signs It's Time for Maintenance

- Drains slower than usual
- Need for frequent plunging
- Minor gurgling sounds
- It's been years since last service
