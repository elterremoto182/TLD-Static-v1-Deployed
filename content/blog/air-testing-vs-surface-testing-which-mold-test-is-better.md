# Air Testing vs Surface Testing: Which Mold Test Is Better?

Neither is universally better—they answer different questions. Air testing shows what spores are in the air you breathe; surface testing shows what is growing on a specific area. For a full picture, professionals often use both. Understanding the difference helps you know what to expect and when each test is appropriate.

## What Air Testing and Surface Testing Do

Air testing collects samples of the air in a room or area and sends them to a lab to measure mold spore counts and types. It answers the question: what are you breathing? It is useful for assessing overall indoor air quality and for comparing indoor levels to outdoor levels (a baseline). Surface testing collects samples from a specific surface—for example, a wall, floor, or piece of furniture—using swabs, tape lifts, or bulk samples. It answers the question: what is growing on this spot? It is useful for confirming mold on a visible stain or for sampling a suspected area behind or inside a wall.

## When to Use One vs. Both

If you have a visible stain and want to confirm it is mold and what type, surface testing may be enough. If you have no visible mold but smell musty odors or had water damage, air testing can show whether spore levels are elevated. For a complete picture—for example, before or after remediation, or when buying or selling—many professionals recommend both: air samples for overall air quality and surface or bulk samples to identify what is growing and where. Your mold testing professional can design a sampling plan based on your situation.

## What the Lab Results Tell You

Air and surface samples are sent to a lab that identifies mold types and reports spore counts or colony growth. The report will list the species found—for example, Aspergillus, Penicillium, or Stachybotrys—and often compare indoor air to outdoor baseline levels. Elevated indoor counts or certain species can indicate a [water damage mold](/problems/water-damage-mold/) or [hidden mold](/problems/hidden-mold/) problem. [Professional mold testing](/mold-testing/) can explain the results and recommend next steps: remediation, moisture control, or clearance testing after cleanup.

## Summary

Neither air nor surface testing is universally better; they answer different questions. For a full assessment after water damage or when hidden mold is suspected, professionals often use both. Schedule [professional mold testing](/mold-testing/) to get the right tests and a clear assessment.
